Computer Hardware: A Student Guide

Explore the essential components that make a computer work.

Core Concepts & Definitions

Input, Output, and Direct Data Entry

Input Devices are hardware components used to send data to a computer. Think of them as the "eyes and ears" of the computer. Examples include a keyboard, a mouse, a microphone, and a webcam.

Output Devices receive data from a computer and display or present it to the user. They are the computer's "mouth." Examples are a monitor (visual output), speakers (audio output), and a printer (physical output).

Direct Data Entry Devices are a specialized type of input device that captures data without the need for human-transcription. They are often used to increase speed and accuracy. Examples include a magnetic stripe reader (used for credit cards), a barcode scanner (used at a grocery store checkout), and an optical mark recognition (OMR) scanner (used for multiple-choice tests).

Storage Media

Storage devices are used to save data for later use. We'll differentiate between three primary types based on their technology.

Type Image How It Works Pros Cons
Magnetic (HDD) Uses a spinning platter with a magnetic head to read/write data. High capacity, low cost per gigabyte, durable for long-term archiving. Slower access speeds, mechanical parts can fail, sensitive to physical shock.
Optical (CD/DVD) Uses a laser to read or write data onto a reflective disc. Very cheap, great for distributing software or music, resistant to magnetic fields. Low capacity, slow read/write speeds, susceptible to scratches and heat.
Solid-State (SSD) Uses flash memory (similar to a USB drive) to store data electronically. Extremely fast read/write speeds, no moving parts, very durable. Higher cost per gigabyte, limited number of write cycles, can lose data without power over long periods.
