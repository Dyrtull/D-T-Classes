Case Study: The Tacoma Narrows Bridge Failure

The Tacoma Narrows Bridge failed because of an unusual force called torsion (twisting) caused by wind, but the final collapse was a dramatic example of compression and tension. As the bridge twisted, the road deck was stretched and pulled (tension) on one side and squeezed (compression) on the other. Eventually, these forces became too large, causing the structure to snap apart. This shows why a designer must calculate the maximum forces a bridge can withstand.

Free-Body Diagrams (FBDs)

How do engineers figure out which parts are in tension or compression? They use Free-Body Diagrams (FBDs) and simple math at each joint.

Free-Body Diagrams (FBDs)

An FBD is a simplified drawing of a single joint, showing all the forces acting on it as arrows. Arrows pointing away from the joint represent tension (pulling), and arrows pointing towards the joint represent compression (pushing). For the bridge to be stable, all forces at a joint must cancel each other out.

The Method of Joints

This method applies a fundamental rule of physics: for an object to be stationary, all forces must be in balance. We apply this at each joint of the truss. We ensure that:

  • The sum of all vertical (up/down) forces is zero (ΣFy = 0).
  • The sum of all horizontal (left/right) forces is zero (ΣFx = 0).

By using these two equations with some trigonometry, we can solve for the force in each bridge member.

Why Joints Matter Most

In a truss bridge, the members are designed to only handle forces at their ends. This means they are only pushed or pulled along their length, which is called an axial force. The joints are the only places where these forces meet and can be transferred from one member to another or to a support.

Engineers make the simplifying assumption that the joints are pins or hinges. This means the members can pivot freely, so there's no bending force (or moment) transferred through the joint. Because of this, a truss is in a state of equilibrium , and we only need to worry about the forces at each of the joints to understand the stress on the entire structure.

Why Triangles Are Key: Trigonometry

Your spaghetti bridge is a Warren Truss, which is a series of triangles. When a force is applied to a diagonal member, it needs to be broken down into its horizontal (x) and vertical (y) components using trigonometry.

SOH CAH TOA for Bridge Building

To find the horizontal and vertical parts of a diagonal force, you use the sine and cosine of the angle of the triangle. For a force F at an angle θ:

Horizontal Component: Fx = F cos(θ)

Vertical Component: Fy = F sin(θ)

This is a crucial step! Once you have the horizontal and vertical components of all forces at a joint, you can use the ΣFx = 0 and ΣFy = 0 equations to solve for the unknown forces.

Example: A Simple Joint

Let's say we have a member with an unknown force FAB at an angle of 45° and a known load of 10 N pushing down. The joint must be in equilibrium.

Knowns:

Load = 10 N (vertical force pushing down)

Angle = 45°


Calculations:

ΣFy = 0: Load + FAB,y = 0

-10 + FAB sin(45°) = 0

FAB sin(45°) = 10

FAB = 10 / sin(45°) = 10 / 0.707 = 14.14 N (Tension)


Now find the horizontal force:

ΣFx = 0: FAB,x + FAC = 0

FAB cos(45°) + FAC = 0

14.14 * 0.707 + FAC = 0

10 + FAC = 0

FAC = -10 N (Compression)

Pratt Truss Analysis: Your Step-by-Step Example

Now, let's put everything together. We'll find the forces in a different kind of bridge called a Pratt truss. This is the exact process engineers follow to make sure their designs are safe.

The Problem

You have a Pratt truss bridge with a central load of 1000 N. The bridge is 8 meters long and 4 meters tall. We need to calculate the force in each of the members.

Step 1: Calculate the Reaction Forces

The first thing an engineer does is find the forces acting on the bridge from the ground up. Because our bridge is symmetrical and the load is exactly in the center, the load is split evenly between the two supports at points A and D.

Total downward force = 1000 N

Total upward force = Must also be 1000 N to keep the bridge from falling.

Force at each support = (Total downward force) / 2


RA = RD = 1000 N / 2 = 500 N

So, the support at A pushes up with 500 N, and the support at D also pushes up with 500 N.

Step 2: Analyze Joint A (The Left Support)

Now we can isolate one part of the bridge and solve for the unknown forces. We'll start with Joint A, where we know the reaction force (RA = 500 N) and have two unknown member forces: FAC (the bottom horizontal member) and FAB (the diagonal member). We assume both are in tension (pointing away from the joint).

First, we need the angle (θ) of the diagonal member AB. We can use the dimensions of the triangle formed by A, C, and B. The side AC is 4 meters (half of the 8m span), and the side CB is 4 meters (the height).


tan(θ) = 4 m / 4 m = 1
θ = arctan(1) = 45°

Vertical Forces (ΣFy = 0)

The upward force from the ground (RA) must be balanced by the downward component of the diagonal member (FAB).

RA + FAB sin(45°) = 0

500 + FAB(0.707) = 0

FAB = -500 / 0.707 = -707 N

The negative sign means our initial assumption was wrong. FAB is actually a COMPRESSION force, pushing into the joint.

Horizontal Forces (ΣFx = 0)

The horizontal member FAC must be balanced by the horizontal component of the diagonal member FAB.

FAC + FAB cos(45°) = 0

FAC + (-707)(0.707) = 0

FAC - 500 = 0

FAC = 500 N

The positive sign means our assumption was correct. FAC is a TENSION force, pulling the joint.

Step 3: Analyze Joint C (The Midpoint)

Next, we move to Joint C to find the force in the vertical member, FCB. At this joint, we have the known force from member AC (FAC = 500 N, tension), and two unknowns: FCB and FCD.

Vertical Forces (ΣFy = 0)

The only vertical force is from the vertical member FCB.

FCB = 0 N

This is an interesting result! Member CB is a zero-force member in this specific case. It's only needed if the load isn't perfectly centered.

Horizontal Forces (ΣFx = 0)

The horizontal force from AC (FAC) must be balanced by the horizontal force from CD (FCD).

FAC + FCD = 0

500 + FCD = 0

FCD = -500 N

The negative sign means FCD is in COMPRESSION.
