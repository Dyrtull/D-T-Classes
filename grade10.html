Week 1: Design & Technology in Society / Need Observation

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand how Design & Technology (D&T) impacts society and be able to identify a real-world need in the community. Students will also learn to distinguish between a "need" and a "want."

SEL Goal: Develop critical observation skills by analyzing daily technology and identifying its benefits and negative aspects. Students will also practice communication and empathy by interviewing a classmate, family member, or teacher about a product frustration.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Introduction to D&T: Design & Technology is a subject focused on applying practical and creative solutions to meet social, user, or technical needs. It's about solving problems and improving lives through engineered and designed products and systems.

Societal Impact: Design and engineering deeply impact society and solve real needs. This refers to the effect a product or technology has, positively or negatively, on communities and everyday life.

Need vs. Want: In product development, it's crucial to distinguish between a "need" (something essential) and a "want" (something desirable). Designers focus on addressing genuine needs.

Activity:

Technology Analysis & Improvement: Select one technology you use daily (e.g., your school bag, a water bottle, a specific app). List two benefits, one concern or negative aspect, and propose one specific improvement. Be ready to share your ideas with a partner.

Possible Homework:

User Interview for Product Frustration: Interview a classmate, family member, or teacher about a product frustration they experience. Summarize their frustration in 1-2 sentences. Based on their input, draft a preliminary 1-paragraph "Need Statement" that describes the problem you could potentially design a solution for. This will prepare you for writing a design brief next week.

Week 2: Observing Need, Design Brief

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Learn to write a clear and precise design brief that outlines a project's goals, intended user, and constraints.

SEL Goal: Practice teamwork and collaboration by working in a team to write a design brief.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Observing & Communicating Needs: To create effective solutions, designers must thoroughly observe, document, and communicate real needs.

Design Brief: A document outlining the goals, intended user, design requirements, and constraints for a project. It is your guiding document.

Specification: Includes detailed technical and functional requirements derived from the design brief.

Activity:

"Better Classroom Chair" Design Brief: Working with your team, write a three-sentence design brief for a "better classroom chair." Ensure your brief clearly states the overall goal of the design, the intended user, and at least two key requirements or constraints.

Possible Homework:

Bridge Impact Research: Research two different bridges in your local area or a famous region (e.g., Golden Gate Bridge, Puente de Boyacá). For each bridge, list two distinct ways it has positively impacted its community. This will prepare you for analyzing products and their features next week.

Week 3: Product Analysis (Advanced) – Evaluate Existing Solutions

Link to Class Presentation
Which Role would you take in a design project?

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Systematically study and compare existing products using the ACCESSFM framework, which stands for Aesthetics, Cost, Customer, Environment, Safety, Size, Function, and Materials/Manufacture.

SEL Goal: Practice communication and presentation skills by discussing and presenting an ACCESSFM analysis with a partner. Students will also reflect on the consequences of design flaws by watching a documentary on a bridge failure and identifying a key lesson learned.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Product Analysis: The systematic study of an existing product’s function, design, and suitability for purpose.

ACCESSFM: A widely used framework for product analysis based on Aesthetics, Cost, Customer, Environment, Safety, Size, Function, and Materials/Manufacture.

Activity:

ACCESSFM Grid for Common Objects: Working in pairs, select two different common objects (e.g., two different types of pens). Complete an ACCESSFM grid for each. Discuss and write down your analysis points for each letter, then be prepared to briefly present your comparison to the class.

Possible Homework:

Bridge Failure Documentary Reflection: Watch a short documentary or video about a famous bridge failure (e.g., Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse). Write one key lesson learned about structural design, materials, or forces that contributed to the failure. This will get you thinking about the physics of structures, which we will discuss next week.

Week 4: Structures & Forces

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand the two primary forces in a bridge: tension (a pulling force) and compression (a pushing force).

SEL Goal: Develop a deeper understanding of real-world impact by researching the positive societal effects of local or famous bridges.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Structure: A system of connected parts designed to support loads.

Load: The force acting on a structure (e.g., the weight of traffic on a bridge).

Tension: A pulling force that stretches an object apart.

Compression: A pushing force that squeezes an object.

Activity & Exercise:

Craft Stick Test: Take a single craft stick. Try to bend it. How much force does it take to cause it to break? Now, take two sticks and push them against each other, end-to-end (compression). Then, lay them side-by-side and hold them, trying to pull them apart (tension). Which force do you think spaghetti will be better at resisting? Why?

Possible Homework:

Truss Types Research: Using an online resource, research the visual differences between a Pratt, a Howe, and a Warren truss. This will give you a head start for next week's lesson on truss designs.

Week 5: The Power of the Triangle & Truss Design

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand why the triangle is a rigid, stable shape and the most important shape in bridge engineering. Students will also learn about different truss designs (Pratt, Howe, and Warren).

SEL Goal: Practice visual communication and critical thinking by sketching truss types and indicating forces with arrows.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Truss: A structure of connected elements (members) that form triangular units.

Why Triangles are Strong: A triangle is a rigid, stable shape that cannot be deformed without changing the length of its sides.

Truss Types: Common designs include the Pratt, Howe, and Warren trusses.

Activity & Exercise:

Sketching Trusses: Sketch each of the three truss types (Pratt, Howe, Warren). Imagine a weight is placed at the center of each bridge. Using different colored pens, draw arrows on your sketches to indicate which members are in tension and which are in compression.

Possible Homework:

Joint Methods Research: Your bridge's joints will be its weakest points. Research three different methods for joining materials (e.g., butt joint, lap joint, gusset plate). Sketch each one and list a pro and con for each. This will get you thinking about how to build a strong bridge with spaghetti and glue.

Week 6: The Spaghetti and the Glue

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Learn about the material properties of spaghetti and the importance of creating strong joints.

SEL Goal: Develop problem-solving skills and resilience through hands-on experimentation, testing different joint methods to determine which is strongest.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Material Science: A single spaghetti strand is brittle, but a bundle acts very differently.

Joints: The connection points between members are the weakest part of a bridge. We'll use hot glue to create strong joints.

Activity & Exercise:

Strength in Bundles: Take a single spaghetti strand and break it. Now, take 10 strands and tape them tightly together. Try to break the bundle. How much more force did it take?

Joint Strength: Experiment with three different methods for joining spaghetti strands with hot glue. Sketch them and test which one is strongest.

Possible Homework:

Simple Load Distribution: Draw a diagram of a simple, flat beam resting on two supports. Now, draw a weight in the center. Use arrows to show the direction of the forces at the supports. How is the weight of the load distributed? This will be a basic introduction to the load calculations we'll do next week.

Week 7: Force Calculations - The Basics

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Begin calculating forces in bridge members using simple diagrams and the concept of equilibrium.

SEL Goal: Develop self-directed learning skills by researching the "Method of Joints" on their own to prepare for the next lesson.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Free-Body Diagrams: A simple drawing of a joint that shows all the forces acting on it.

Equilibrium: A state where all forces acting on a structure are balanced.

Detailed Example: Let's calculate the force (F) in the members of a simple triangular truss. If a weight W=100 N is applied at the top joint and the angle is $\theta=60^\circ$, we can use the formula F=fracW2sin(theta).

Activity & Exercise:

On a provided worksheet, solve for the force in the two angled members of a triangular truss where the angle $\theta$ is $45^\circ$ and the weight $W$ is 150 N. Make sure you label which members are in tension and which are in compression.

Possible Homework:

Khan Academy Review: Research the "Method of Joints" on Khan Academy or another online resource. Find a simple example and try to follow the steps to solve it. This will prepare you for next week's more complex calculations.

Week 8: Force Calculations - The Method of Joints

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Apply the "Method of Joints" to solve for forces in a more complex truss.

SEL Goal: Use rapid, freehand sketching to visually communicate and document their design ideas.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Method of Joints: A powerful tool that involves isolating each joint in a truss and applying the principles of equilibrium to find the unknown forces.

Activity & Exercise:

We will work through a step-by-step example on the board with a small, two-triangle truss. You will then be given a worksheet with a slightly more complex truss diagram (e.g., a Pratt truss with three triangles) to solve for all the member forces.

Possible Homework:

Truss Joint Sketches with Notes: For your upcoming bridge model, produce three different sketches of potential truss joints. For each sketch, use freehand and rapid sketching techniques and annotate each with notes explaining the materials you would use, how the members would connect, and any advantages or disadvantages.

Term 2

Week 1: Mechanisms & Levers (Class 1)

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Identify the three classes of levers and calculate mechanical advantage (MA) and torque.

Activity: Hands-on experiment to build and test a Class 1 lever, calculating load, effort, and MA for different fulcrum positions.

Key Concepts:

Lever: A rigid bar rotating around a fixed point (fulcrum).
Torque (tau): Rotational force; tau = Force x Distance from axis.
Mechanical Advantage (MA): MA = distance from fulcrum to effort / distance from fulcrum to load.

Week 2: Levers (Class 2 & 3) & Linkages

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Distinguish all three lever classes and understand that Class 3 levers trade force for speed/distance. Introduce linkages for motion transfer.

Activity: Build a simple 4-bar linkage and observe how motion is transferred and changed.

Key Concepts:

Class 2 Lever: Load in the middle (MA > 1).
Class 3 Lever: Effort in the middle (MA < 1, trades force for speed).
Linkage: A series of connected levers that transfer motion.

Week 3: Velocity Ratio, Efficiency & Torque

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand and calculate Velocity Ratio (VR) and Efficiency of mechanisms. Introduce Torsion (twisting force).

Activity: Test single fixed and movable pulley systems to measure actual MA, calculate VR, and determine efficiency loss due to friction.

Key Concepts:

Velocity Ratio (VR): Distance moved by effort / distance moved by load.
Efficiency: (Actual MA / VR) x 100%.
Torsion: Twisting deformation that stores elastic potential energy.

Week 4: Types of Motion & Conversion

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Identify and describe rotary, linear, reciprocating, and oscillating motion. Understand how crank and slider mechanisms convert motion.

Activity: Build a crank and slider mechanism to convert rotary input into reciprocating linear motion, and analyze the motion's speed changes.

Key Concepts:

Rotary: Movement in circles.
Reciprocating: Back-and-forth linear motion.
Crank and Slider: Converts rotary motion to reciprocating motion (e.g., car piston).

Week 5: Cams & Cam Followers

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand cam profiles (eccentric, pear, snail) and their effects on follower motion (rise, dwell, fall).

Activity: Build and test three different cam profiles, measuring follower height at various rotation angles and graphing the displacement.

Key Concepts:

Cam: An irregular rotating shape that generates specific motion.
Follower: The part that moves in contact with the cam.
Dwell: Period of cam rotation where the follower is stationary.

Week 6: Gears & Gear Ratios

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand how gears transmit motion, calculate gear ratios, and distinguish between spur, bevel, and worm gears.

Activity: Calculate output speed and torque for simple gear trains and physically test the speed vs. torque trade-off using gear sets.

Key Concepts:

Gear Ratio (GR): Driven Teeth / Driver Teeth (trades speed for torque).
Torque Multiplication: Output Torque = Input Torque x GR.
Spur Gears: Connect parallel shafts; teeth are straight.

Week 7: Compound Gear Trains

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand and calculate the overall gear ratio for compound gear trains (multiple stages). Apply knowledge to design winch systems.

Activity: Build a compound gear train and verify the calculated overall gear ratio by counting input and output rotations.

Key Concepts:

Compound Gear Train: Two or more gears share the same shaft.
Overall GR: GR Stage 1 x GR Stage 2.
Winch Application: High GR provides the high torque needed to lift heavy loads slowly.

Week 8: Belt Drives & Rack-and-Pinion

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand belt drive advantages, calculate their velocity ratio, and recognize rack-and-pinion motion conversion (Rotary -> Linear).

Activity: Build and test a belt drive system with spools of different diameters, comparing calculated VR to observed rotation.

Key Concepts:

Belt VR: Driven Diameter / Driver Diameter (for pulleys).
Advantage: Transmits motion over long distances; acts as a shock absorber.
Rack-and-Pinion: Converts the rotary motion of the pinion gear to the linear motion of the rack (e.g., car steering).

Week 9: Circuit Fundamentals & Series

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand voltage (V), current (I), and resistance (R). Apply Ohm's Law and build series circuits.

Activity: Build and calculate current for a single-LED series circuit, understanding why a resistor is necessary to protect the LED.

Key Concepts:

Ohm's Law: V = I x R.
Series Circuit: Components in a single loop; Current is the same throughout; Voltage divides.
LED Resistor: R = (V_battery - V_LED) / I_desired.

Week 10: Parallel Circuits & Switches

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Build and analyze parallel circuits and compare their advantages to series circuits.

Activity: Build a parallel circuit with two independently switched LED branches to demonstrate that one failure does not affect the other components.

Key Concepts:

Parallel Circuit: Components connected across common voltage points; Voltage is the same across all; Current divides.
Advantage: Reliability and independent control (e.g., house wiring).

Week 11: Project Design & Build Phase 1

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Synthesize all mechanisms knowledge to design a trebuchet/catapult and create technical drawings with calculated specifications (MA, Torque, Energy).

Activity: Team brainstorming, design selection (Spring, Counterweight, or Hybrid), preliminary calculations for energy transfer, and beginning construction.

Key Concepts:

Design Synthesis: Applying multiple concepts (levers, torque, ratios) into one system.
Energy Transfer: Converting stored potential energy (spring/counterweight) into projectile kinetic energy.
Documentation: Technical drawings, MA, and torque calculations are required.

Week 12: Build, Test & Competition

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Execute construction plan, test and iterate the design, and calculate actual efficiency from launch data.

Activity: Final build completion, inter-grade competition (distance and accuracy challenges), and post-competition failure analysis.

Key Concepts:

Iterative Design: Testing, observing failure, and making improvements.
Troubleshooting: Applying physics principles to correct structural or mechanical faults.
Performance Data: Comparing calculated predictions to actual test results.

Term 3

Week 1: Electronics II - Transistors Introduction

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand transistors (NPN BJT) as electronic switches controlled by a small current at the Base pin.

Activity: Build a simple transistor circuit where a push button supplies current to the Base to switch on a larger current to an LED (Current Gain demonstration).

Key Concepts:

Transistor (NPN): Switch with three pins: Collector (C), Base (B), Emitter (E).
Switching: Small current at B allows large current from C to E.
Application: Controlling high-power devices (motors, relays) with low-power signals.

Week 2: Relays & Sensor Integration

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand relay operation (electrically operated switch) and integrate sensors like the LDR (Light Dependent Resistor) into control systems.

Activity: Build a light-activated circuit using an LDR and a transistor to automatically turn an LED ON when it gets dark, and a separate circuit where a transistor switches a relay to control a motor.

Key Concepts:

Relay: Isolates control circuit from high-power load circuit.
LDR: Resistance decreases when light increases; used in a voltage divider to detect light level.
Voltage Divider: Converts sensor resistance change into a voltage signal to trigger the transistor.

Week 3: Motor Control & PWM

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand how an H-bridge controls motor direction and how Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) controls motor speed.

Activity: Build a simple H-bridge circuit with manual switches to control motor direction. Observe a demonstration of PWM changing the motor's speed without changing the supply voltage.

Key Concepts:

H-Bridge: Uses four switches to control the polarity of voltage across a motor, changing its direction.
PWM: Rapid ON/OFF switching to create an average voltage, controlling speed. Duty Cycle: Percentage of time the signal is ON (determines average speed).

Week 4: Final Electronics Integration

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Combine sensors, transistors, relays, and motors to design and build a functional automatic control system.

Activity: Design and build a small system (e.g., a temperature-controlled fan or a light-activated night light) using all learned components. Document the circuit with a schematic.

Key Concepts:

System Integration: Creating a cohesive whole from different components (Input -> Processing -> Output).
Schematic Diagram: Formal representation of an electronic circuit using standardized symbols.
Troubleshooting: Identifying and fixing errors in complex circuits.

Week 5: CAD/CAM I - TinkerCAD Basics

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand CAD (Computer-Aided Design) and master the TinkerCAD interface. Learn to create precise dimensions and shapes.

Activity: TinkerCAD tutorial to create a simple house model and a precision gear mounting bracket using Solid and Hole shapes, and the Align tool.

Key Concepts:

CAD: Digital creation of 2D drawings and 3D models with perfect accuracy.
Workplane: The 3D surface where modeling occurs.
Hole: A shape used to subtract material from a solid model upon Grouping.

Week 6: CAD/CAM II - 2D Extrusion

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Create custom 2D profiles (sketches) and convert them into 3D objects using extrusion. Understand design principles for laser cutting.

Activity: Design a decorative gear and a personalized name tag in TinkerCAD, modeling the process of drawing a 2D vector path and pulling it up to a specific thickness.

Key Concepts:

Extrusion: Creating a 3D solid by pulling a 2D profile perpendicular to the plane.
Laser Cutting: A 2D subtractive process; design uses DXF/SVG vector files.
Kerf: The width of the material removed by the laser (must be compensated for joints).

Week 7: CAD/CAM III - Assemblies & Fit

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Design assemblies where multiple parts fit together. Understand the concept of clearance (space between parts for movement) and interference (friction fit).

Activity: Design a simple hinged box assembly and a gear and axle assembly, ensuring that the holes are slightly larger than the pins or axles (e.g., 5.2mm hole for a 5mm pin) for a clearance fit.

Key Concepts:

Assembly: Multiple modeled parts designed to fit together.
Clearance Fit: Hole is slightly larger than the shaft to allow free movement (e.g., for rotation).
Interference Fit: Shaft is slightly larger than the hole for a tight, friction-based connection.

Week 8: CAD for Mechanisms

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Model complex mechanism components like cams (eccentric, pear) and linkage systems digitally using precise measurements.

Activity: Model a pear-shaped cam with an axle hole and design the parts for a slider-crank mechanism, verifying that all pins and holes allow movement.

Key Concepts:

Mechanism Modeling: Using CAD to test fit and function of moving parts before physical construction.
Cam Design: Precisely modeling the cam profile to ensure the correct rise, dwell, and fall of the follower.

Week 9: CAM - Export for Manufacturing

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand the CAM (Computer-Aided Manufacturing) workflow and prepare files for different machines by exporting to STL (3D printing) or DXF/SVG (laser cutting).

Activity: Export a gear model as an STL file and conceptually walk through the slicing process (layer height, infill). Design a coaster and prepare the file for laser cutting (distinguishing cut vs engrave lines).

Key Concepts:

CAM: Translates CAD models into machine instructions (G-code).
STL: Standard file format for 3D printing (defines surface geometry).
Slicer: Software that converts STL into G-code (machine path instructions).

Week 10: Final Project Design (Automaton 2.0)

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Synthesize all learning to design an Electro-Mechanical Automaton 2.0 that combines at least two mechanism types, a motor, sensors, and one CAD-designed part.

Activity: Team brainstorming, concept selection, drawing a system block diagram (Input -> Processing -> Output), and identifying the custom CAD part to be modeled.

Key Concepts:

Design Synthesis: The final project requires integrating mechanical, electronic, and digital (CAD) skills.
System Block Diagram: Visual map of how input sensors trigger processing (transistor/relay) to control output (motor/mechanism).

Week 11: Final Project Build & CAD

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Construct the mechanical framework, wire and test the electronic circuit, and complete the CAD model of the custom part (ready for manufacturing/template cutting).

Activity: Phased build: 1. Mechanical construction (motor, gears, cams); 2. Electronic assembly (breadboard test, final wiring); 3. CAD model completion and export. Integration testing begins.

Key Concepts:

Phased Construction: Building and testing subsystems before final assembly.
Integration Testing: Verifying that the electronic output correctly drives the mechanical input.
CAD for Manufacturing: Using the exported STL/DXF to create the physical part.

Week 12: Final Assembly & Presentations

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Complete the integrated system, write the final technical documentation (schematics, BOM, explanation), and present the functioning project.

Activity: Final optimization for smooth motion and reliable electronics. Project Presentations (demo and technical explanation) and reflection on the Term 2/3 learning journey.

Key Concepts:

Optimization: Fine-tuning performance (e.g., reducing friction, calibrating sensor thresholds).
Technical Documentation: Required for assessment (drawings, schematics, Bill of Materials, operation explanation).
Summative Assessment: Project is the final assessment for the terms, covering all unit goals.
