Week 1: Design & Technology in Society / Need Observation

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand how Design & Technology (D&T) impacts society and be able to identify a real-world need in the community. Students will also learn to distinguish between a "need" and a "want."

SEL Goal: Develop critical observation skills by analyzing daily technology and identifying its benefits and negative aspects. Students will also practice communication and empathy by interviewing a classmate, family member, or teacher about a product frustration.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Introduction to D&T: Design & Technology is a subject focused on applying practical and creative solutions to meet social, user, or technical needs. It's about solving problems and improving lives through engineered and designed products and systems.

Societal Impact: Design and engineering deeply impact society and solve real needs. This refers to the effect a product or technology has, positively or negatively, on communities and everyday life.

Need vs. Want: In product development, it's crucial to distinguish between a "need" (something essential) and a "want" (something desirable). Designers focus on addressing genuine needs.

Activity:

Technology Analysis & Improvement: Select one technology you use daily (e.g., your school bag, a water bottle, a specific app). List two benefits, one concern or negative aspect, and propose one specific improvement. Be ready to share your ideas with a partner.

Possible Homework:

User Interview for Product Frustration: Interview a classmate, family member, or teacher about a product frustration they experience. Summarize their frustration in 1-2 sentences. Based on their input, draft a preliminary 1-paragraph "Need Statement" that describes the problem you could potentially design a solution for. This will prepare you for writing a design brief next week.

Week 2: Observing Need, Design Brief

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Learn to write a clear and precise design brief that outlines a project's goals, intended user, and constraints.

SEL Goal: Practice teamwork and collaboration by working in a team to write a design brief.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Observing & Communicating Needs: To create effective solutions, designers must thoroughly observe, document, and communicate real needs.

Design Brief: A document outlining the goals, intended user, design requirements, and constraints for a project. It is your guiding document.

Specification: Includes detailed technical and functional requirements derived from the design brief.

Activity:

"Better Classroom Chair" Design Brief: Working with your team, write a three-sentence design brief for a "better classroom chair." Ensure your brief clearly states the overall goal of the design, the intended user, and at least two key requirements or constraints.

Possible Homework:

Bridge Impact Research: Research two different bridges in your local area or a famous region (e.g., Golden Gate Bridge, Puente de Boyacá). For each bridge, list two distinct ways it has positively impacted its community. This will prepare you for analyzing products and their features next week.

Week 3: Product Analysis (Advanced) – Evaluate Existing Solutions

Link to Class Presentation
Which Role would you take in a design project?

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Systematically study and compare existing products using the ACCESSFM framework, which stands for Aesthetics, Cost, Customer, Environment, Safety, Size, Function, and Materials/Manufacture.

SEL Goal: Practice communication and presentation skills by discussing and presenting an ACCESSFM analysis with a partner. Students will also reflect on the consequences of design flaws by watching a documentary on a bridge failure and identifying a key lesson learned.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Product Analysis: The systematic study of an existing product’s function, design, and suitability for purpose.

ACCESSFM: A widely used framework for product analysis based on Aesthetics, Cost, Customer, Environment, Safety, Size, Function, and Materials/Manufacture.

Activity:

ACCESSFM Grid for Common Objects: Working in pairs, select two different common objects (e.g., two different types of pens). Complete an ACCESSFM grid for each. Discuss and write down your analysis points for each letter, then be prepared to briefly present your comparison to the class.

Possible Homework:

Bridge Failure Documentary Reflection: Watch a short documentary or video about a famous bridge failure (e.g., Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse). Write one key lesson learned about structural design, materials, or forces that contributed to the failure. This will get you thinking about the physics of structures, which we will discuss next week.

Week 4: Structures & Forces

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand the two primary forces in a bridge: tension (a pulling force) and compression (a pushing force).

SEL Goal: Develop a deeper understanding of real-world impact by researching the positive societal effects of local or famous bridges.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Structure: A system of connected parts designed to support loads.

Load: The force acting on a structure (e.g., the weight of traffic on a bridge).

Tension: A pulling force that stretches an object apart.

Compression: A pushing force that squeezes an object.

Activity & Exercise:

Craft Stick Test: Take a single craft stick. Try to bend it. How much force does it take to cause it to break? Now, take two sticks and push them against each other, end-to-end (compression). Then, lay them side-by-side and hold them, trying to pull them apart (tension). Which force do you think spaghetti will be better at resisting? Why?

Possible Homework:

Truss Types Research: Using an online resource, research the visual differences between a Pratt, a Howe, and a Warren truss. This will give you a head start for next week's lesson on truss designs.

Week 5: The Power of the Triangle & Truss Design

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Understand why the triangle is a rigid, stable shape and the most important shape in bridge engineering. Students will also learn about different truss designs (Pratt, Howe, and Warren).

SEL Goal: Practice visual communication and critical thinking by sketching truss types and indicating forces with arrows.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Truss: A structure of connected elements (members) that form triangular units.

Why Triangles are Strong: A triangle is a rigid, stable shape that cannot be deformed without changing the length of its sides.

Truss Types: Common designs include the Pratt, Howe, and Warren trusses.

Activity & Exercise:

Sketching Trusses: Sketch each of the three truss types (Pratt, Howe, Warren). Imagine a weight is placed at the center of each bridge. Using different colored pens, draw arrows on your sketches to indicate which members are in tension and which are in compression.

Possible Homework:

Joint Methods Research: Your bridge's joints will be its weakest points. Research three different methods for joining materials (e.g., butt joint, lap joint, gusset plate). Sketch each one and list a pro and con for each. This will get you thinking about how to build a strong bridge with spaghetti and glue.

Week 6: The Spaghetti and the Glue

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Learn about the material properties of spaghetti and the importance of creating strong joints.

SEL Goal: Develop problem-solving skills and resilience through hands-on experimentation, testing different joint methods to determine which is strongest.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Material Science: A single spaghetti strand is brittle, but a bundle acts very differently.

Joints: The connection points between members are the weakest part of a bridge. We'll use hot glue to create strong joints.

Activity & Exercise:

Strength in Bundles: Take a single spaghetti strand and break it. Now, take 10 strands and tape them tightly together. Try to break the bundle. How much more force did it take?

Joint Strength: Experiment with three different methods for joining spaghetti strands with hot glue. Sketch them and test which one is strongest.

Possible Homework:

Simple Load Distribution: Draw a diagram of a simple, flat beam resting on two supports. Now, draw a weight in the center. Use arrows to show the direction of the forces at the supports. How is the weight of the load distributed? This will be a basic introduction to the load calculations we'll do next week.

Week 7: Force Calculations - The Basics

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Begin calculating forces in bridge members using simple diagrams and the concept of equilibrium.

SEL Goal: Develop self-directed learning skills by researching the "Method of Joints" on their own to prepare for the next lesson.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Free-Body Diagrams: A simple drawing of a joint that shows all the forces acting on it.

Equilibrium: A state where all forces acting on a structure are balanced.

Detailed Example: Let's calculate the force (F) in the members of a simple triangular truss. If a weight W=100 N is applied at the top joint and the angle is $\theta=60^\circ$, we can use the formula F=fracW2sin(theta).

Activity & Exercise:

On a provided worksheet, solve for the force in the two angled members of a triangular truss where the angle $\theta$ is $45^\circ$ and the weight $W$ is 150 N. Make sure you label which members are in tension and which are in compression.

Possible Homework:

Khan Academy Review: Research the "Method of Joints" on Khan Academy or another online resource. Find a simple example and try to follow the steps to solve it. This will prepare you for next week's more complex calculations.

Week 8: Force Calculations - The Method of Joints

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Apply the "Method of Joints" to solve for forces in a more complex truss.

SEL Goal: Use rapid, freehand sketching to visually communicate and document their design ideas.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Method of Joints: A powerful tool that involves isolating each joint in a truss and applying the principles of equilibrium to find the unknown forces.

Activity & Exercise:

We will work through a step-by-step example on the board with a small, two-triangle truss. You will then be given a worksheet with a slightly more complex truss diagram (e.g., a Pratt truss with three triangles) to solve for all the member forces.

Possible Homework:

Truss Joint Sketches with Notes: For your upcoming bridge model, produce three different sketches of potential truss joints. For each sketch, use freehand and rapid sketching techniques and annotate each with notes explaining the materials you would use, how the members would connect, and any advantages or disadvantages.

Week 9: Sketchbook Skills – Sketching, Annotation

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Effectively communicate design ideas through annotated sketches.

SEL Goal: Take ownership of their design by finalizing their bridge and drawing a detailed sketch with dimensions and force diagrams.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Visual Communication: Using sketching to quickly generate, explore, and communicate design ideas.

Annotation: Adding labels, notes, and explanations to your drawings to clarify your design intent.

Activity:

Truss Joint Sketches with Notes: You will be working on a truss/bridge model. Produce three different sketches of potential truss joints. For each sketch, use freehand and rapid sketching techniques and annotate each with notes explaining the materials you would use, how the members would connect, and any advantages or disadvantages.

Possible Homework:

Final Design Options: Based on the forces and concepts you've learned, finalize your bridge design. Draw a final, detailed sketch of the entire bridge, including dimensions and a diagram showing which members will be in tension and compression. This will prepare you for creating your blueprint next week.

Week 10: Blueprint and Final Design

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Create a detailed, scaled blueprint for bridge construction.

SEL Goal: Develop planning and organizational skills by writing a step-by-step construction plan and creating a detailed material list.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Blueprint: A precise, scaled drawing that serves as the guide for your build.

Prototyping: The process of creating preliminary models to test ideas.

Activity:

Drafting Your Blueprint: Based on all your knowledge, finalize your bridge design. Your blueprint must be drawn to scale and include overall dimensions, joint locations, and a diagram showing which members are in tension (T) and which are in compression (C).

Possible Homework:

Construction Plan & Material List: Write a step-by-step plan for building your bridge. Include a detailed list of all the materials you will need, including the number of spaghetti strands for each member and the approximate amount of glue. This will get you ready for the hands-on session next week.

Week 11: The Construction Phase

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Apply knowledge from blueprints to construct the physical bridge with accuracy.

SEL Goal: Develop attention to detail and patience by ensuring measurements are careful and glue is allowed to dry completely. Students will also engage in critical thinking by hypothesizing where their bridge might fail and why.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Accuracy: The degree to which your physical model matches your intended design.

Quality Control: Checking your work as you build to ensure it meets requirements.

Activity:

Build! It's time to bring your blueprint to life. Use your blueprint as a guide, and remember to measure carefully and allow your glue to dry completely. A small mistake in measuring or gluing can weaken the entire structure.

Possible Homework:

Failure Hypothesis: Based on your design, the materials, and your force calculations, write a short hypothesis (2-3 sentences) about where you think your bridge will fail when tested and why.

Week 12: Testing Day

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Analyze why and where a structure fails in a real-world test.

SEL Goal: Develop resilience and the ability to learn from failure by observing the structural test and taking notes on the failure point.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Structural Test: Applying loads to a physical model to observe its behavior and determine its limits.

Failure Analysis: Observing why and where a structure fails to inform future designs.

Activity & Report:

The Grand Finale: We will test your bridge by adding weights until it fails. Take notes on the failure point.

Final Report: After testing, you will complete a short report detailing your bridge's strength, where and why it failed, and how your calculations compared to the real-world results.

Possible Homework:

Comprehensive Concept Review: Review all your notes, handouts, and returned assignments from Weeks 1-12. Pay particular attention to key definitions, examples, and the steps for processes like the design cycle or ACCESSFM. This will prepare you for the final quiz and reflective essay.

Week 13: Summative Assessment & Final Reflection

Link to Class Presentation

Goals:

Cognitive Goal: Synthesize and demonstrate understanding of key concepts from the entire term through a comprehensive quiz.

SEL Goal: Reflect on personal growth and skill development throughout the project by writing a reflective essay.

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Summative Assessment: A comprehensive quiz designed to measure your understanding of the term's key concepts.

Reflecting on Growth: Considering what you have learned, how your understanding has developed, and how your skills have improved throughout the project.

Activity & Essay:

Quiz Practice: Participate in a short practice quiz covering key concepts, basic calculations, and diagram interpretation.

Reflective Essay: Write a reflective essay based on prompts you have been given, such as: "How has your understanding of how forces act on structures changed from the beginning of this term to now?"
