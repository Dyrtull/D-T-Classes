Term 1

Week 1: Design & Technology in Society / Need Observation

Class Presentation

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Introduction to D&T: Design & Technology (D&T) is a subject focused on applying practical and creative solutions to meet social, user, or technical needs. It's about solving problems and improving lives through engineered and designed products and systems.

Societal Impact: Design and engineering deeply impact society and solve real needs. This refers to the effect a product or technology has, positively or negatively, on communities and everyday life.

Need vs. Want: In product development, it's crucial to distinguish between a "need" (an essential problem) and a "want" (something desirable but not essential).

Examples:

Technology Impacting Society: Positive Impact: Mobile phones have positively impacted society by enabling instant global communication, access to information, and emergency services. Negative Impact: Mobile phones can also lead to concerns like digital addiction, privacy issues, and the environmental impact of electronic waste.

Need vs. Want: Need: Students need a comfortable, stable surface to work on in a classroom. Want: Students might want a chair that reclines, has built-in speakers, and vibrates.

In-Class Exercise:

Technology Analysis & Improvement: Select one technology you use daily. List two benefits and one concern related to its use. Propose one specific improvement. Be ready to share your ideas with a partner.

Possible Homework:

Bridge Impact Research: Research two different bridges. For each, list two distinct ways it has positively impacted its community.

Week 2: Observing Need, Design Brief

Link to Class Presentation

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Observing & Communicating Needs: To create effective solutions, designers must thoroughly observe, document, and communicate real needs.

Design Brief: A document outlining the goals, intended user, design requirements, constraints, and outcomes for a project. It serves as a guiding document before starting the actual design process.

Examples:

Observing Need: Students often struggle to carry multiple books and a laptop comfortably between classes...

Design Brief (for a "better classroom chair"): Project Title: Ergonomic Classroom Chair. Goal: To design and prototype a classroom chair that improves student comfort and posture...

In-Class Exercise:

"Better Classroom Chair" Design Brief: Working with your team, write a three-sentence design brief for a "better classroom chair."

Possible Homework:

User Interview for Product Frustration: Interview a user about a product frustration, summarize their frustration, and draft a preliminary "Need Statement."

Week 3: Product Analysis (Advanced) – Evaluate Existing Solutions

Link to Class Presentation

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Product Analysis: The systematic study of an existing product’s function, design, and suitability for purpose.

ACCESSFM: A framework for product analysis covering Aesthetics, Cost, Customer, Environment, Safety, Size, Function, and Materials/Manufacture.

Examples:

ACCESSFM for a Simple Water Bottle: Aesthetics: Sleek, translucent plastic... Cost: $10 to buy... etc.

In-Class Exercise:

ACCESSFM Grid for Common Objects: Work in pairs to select two common objects and complete an ACCESSFM grid for each.

Possible Homework:

Product Review Video Analysis: Watch two professional product review videos and list three comparison points, relating them to the ACCESSFM framework.

Week 4: Sketchbook Skills – Sketching, Annotation

Link to Class Presentation

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Visual Communication in Design: Sketching is a crucial tool for designers to quickly generate, explore, and communicate ideas.

Annotation: The process of adding labels, notes, or explanations to drawings to clarify meaning.

Examples:

Annotated Sketch: Imagine sketching a new chair design...

Isometric Sketch: A simple cube drawn in isometric perspective...

Sketch Photography & Reflection: Photograph your best sketch and add a brief comment explaining what you like and one thing you would improve.

Week 5: Structures Theory I – Bridge Types, Classic Cases

Link to Class Presentation

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Structure: A system of connected parts designed to support loads or forces.

Force: A push or pull exerted on an object. Main types include Tension, Compression, Shear, Bending, and Torsion.

Equilibrium: A state where all forces acting on a structure are balanced.

Examples:

Identifying Forces in a Bridge: Suspension Bridge (e.g., Golden Gate Bridge): The main cables are under immense tension... Arch Bridge: The arch shape efficiently transfers vertical loads... Beam Bridge: The top of the beam is under compression...

In-Class Exercise:

Truss Bridge Diagram & Forces: Draw a simple truss bridge, label its parts, and use arrows to indicate whether key members are experiencing tension or compression under a load.

Possible Homework:

Bridge Failure Documentary Reflection: Watch a video about a famous bridge failure and write a key lesson learned. Also, identify forces in two different everyday objects.

Week 6: Structures Theory II – Plan/Model Truss/Bridge

Link to Class Presentation

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Truss: A structure of connected elements that form triangular units, making it highly efficient at distributing loads.

Truss Bridge Parts: Chords, Web Members, Joint/Node, and Deck.

Stress & Strain: Stress is force per unit area; strain is the deformation in response to stress.

Examples:

Truss Types (Visual): Examples of common truss types like Pratt, Howe, Warren, and K-truss will be shown...

Force Diagram for a Simple Truss: Imagine a simple triangular truss with a weight applied to its top joint...

In-Class Exercise:

Force Diagram with Central Load: Draw a force diagram for a simple symmetrical truss with a central load, indicating tension and compression in key members.

Possible Homework:

Unusual Bridge Research: Research an unusual bridge, describe its unique feature, and explain how it manages forces.

Week 7: Model Building – Start Model Construction

Link to Class Presentation

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Prototyping in Engineering: The process of creating preliminary models to test ideas, identify flaws, and refine designs.

Safety in Model Building: Understanding proper tool usage and wearing appropriate PPE to avoid accidents.

Examples:

Prototyping: Before building a full-size bridge, engineers might build a scale model to test its strength and stability...

Safety Rule Application: When using a craft knife to cut wood strips, always cut away from your body, use a cutting mat, and keep your fingers clear of the blade.

In-Class Exercise:

Team Model Building Checklist: As a team, create a checklist of at least five crucial steps to ensure accurate and safe model building.

Possible Homework:

Model Progress & Challenge Note: Take a photo of your model's progress and write a short note describing a challenge your team faced and how you plan to address it.

Week 8: Project Synthesis – Submit Analysis, Sketches, Model

Link to Class Presentation

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Synthesis: Bringing together all parts of your project into a unified final product or presentation.

Peer & Self Assessment: Classmates and you evaluate your projects based on predefined criteria, providing feedback and developing critical thinking skills.

Examples:

Project Synthesis Components: Your final submission might include: The original design brief, Product analysis (ACCESSFM) of existing solutions...

Using a Rubric for Peer Assessment: A rubric might have criteria like "Clarity of Design Brief," "Quality of Sketches," "Structural Integrity of Model," "Presentation Skills," with different levels of achievement.

In-Class Exercise:

Group Rubric & "One Thing to Improve": Review the project rubric as a group and use it to identify one specific thing your group could most improve before the final submission.

Possible Homework:

Bridge-Building Contest Video Analysis: Watch a video of a contest and note three specific strategies or design elements that contributed to the winning teams' success.

Week 9: Structural Critique – Testing & Peer Critique

Link to Class Presentation

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Structural Test: Applying loads to a model to observe how it behaves and determine its limits before failure.

Methods of Critique: A systematic and constructive evaluation of a design to identify areas for improvement and celebrate successes.

Examples:

Stress Testing: Your bridge model is placed on a testing jig...

Guided Peer Critique: After a bridge test, peers might comment: "Your arch members showed good compression resistance..."

In-Class Exercise:

Written Reflection: "What Worked? What Would Improve Stability?": After testing, reflect on what worked well and identify one specific area of weakness, proposing a design change to improve it.

Possible Homework:

Improvements After Testing: Based on observations and feedback, list three specific improvements you would make to your bridge model's design or construction.

Week 10: Evaluate & Iterate – Revise Design/Model

Link to Class Presentation

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Iteration: The repeating cycle of testing, critiquing, and improving a product or design.

Engineering Redesign: The process of using lessons learned from failures and feedback to create better, more robust products.

Examples:

Iterative Design in Action: Version 1 (Prototype): Your bridge model holds 5kg before breaking...

In-Class Exercise:

Design Changes & Explanation: Based on testing results, list and explain at least two specific design changes you have made or plan to make to your model, and state why you expect the change to improve its performance.

Possible Homework:

Redesign Scenarios: Describe one specific design change you would make to address scenarios like carrying a wider load or using half the material.

Week 11: Final Build – Polish, Last Peer Review

Link to Class Presentation

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Final Steps & Documentation: The final stage of a project involves completing, checking, and thoroughly documenting your work.

Quality Assurance/Control: Systematic checks made throughout the design and build process to ensure the final product meets requirements and is safe.

Examples:

Polishing the Model: Sanding any rough edges on the wood strips...

Self-Assessment Checklist: A checklist might include items like: "All measurements are accurate within 1mm."

In-Class Exercise:

Model Quality Criteria Rating: Rate your final bridge model and documentation on five key quality criteria provided by the teacher, and provide a brief justification for your rating.

Possible Homework:

Presentation Slides for Extra Credit: Prepare a short set of presentation slides that summarize your bridge project, including the original design brief, key features, photos, and test results.

Week 12: Summative Assessment – Quiz & Reflective Essay

Link to Class Presentation

Theory Concepts & Definitions:

Review of Term Concepts: A review of all the concepts covered throughout the term, from the design cycle to structural forces.

Summative Assessment: A comprehensive test to measure your understanding of the term’s key concepts and your ability to apply them.

Examples:

Quiz Questions: "Define 'compression' and give an example of a structural member that typically experiences this force."

Quiz Practice: Participate in a short practice quiz covering key concepts, basic calculations, and diagram interpretation.

Comprehensive Concept Review: Review all your notes and handouts, paying particular attention to key definitions, examples, and processes like the design cycle or ACCESSFM.
