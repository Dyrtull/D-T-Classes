Introduction: The Importance of Joints

In our last class, we learned how to analyze the forces on a truss bridge. Today, we're going to focus on a critical but often overlooked part of any structure: the joints. A building is only as strong as its weakest point, and that's usually where the pieces are connected. We will act like engineers and experiment with different ways to connect two pieces of spaghetti to find the strongest method.

Hands-On Joint Experimentation

Now it's time for our main challenge! In your groups, you will become engineers and test different methods for joining two pieces of spaghetti. Your goal is to create the strongest joint possible.

Class Findings: What Makes a Strong Joint?

Now that you've all had a chance to experiment, let's share what we discovered. Each group will briefly present their best joint and the weight it held. We will then discuss the common methods that worked and why they were successful.

Key Concepts:

  • Surface Area: The more contact area between the two pieces of spaghetti and the glue, the stronger the joint will be. Think about the difference between a butt joint (end-to-end) and a lap joint (overlapping).
  • Reinforcement: Materials like string or tape help to distribute the force, preventing a single point of failure. This is a form of reinforcement.
  • Load Distribution: Strong joints are designed to handle the different types of forces (or stresses) that will be applied to them.
  • Material Properties:
    • Brittleness: The tendency of a material to break without significant deformation. Spaghetti is a great example of a brittle material.
    • Elasticity: A material's ability to return to its original shape after being stretched or compressed.
  • Types of Stress: Beyond the tension and compression we learned about, joints also experience other stresses:
    • Shear Stress: A force that causes a sliding motion between two parts.
    • Torsion: A twisting force.
