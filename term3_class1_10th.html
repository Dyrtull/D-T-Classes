⚡ The Three Heroes of Every Circuit

All electronics work because of three quantities that always interact: Voltage, Current, and Resistance. Think of them like a garden hose.

The Simple Story

Quantity What It Is Unit Garden Hose Analogy
Voltage (V) The electrical "push" or pressure Volt (V) How hard you squeeze the tap
Current (I) How many electrons flow per second Ampere (A) How much water comes out
Resistance (R) Opposition to electron flow Ohm (Ω) How kinked the hose is

Key Idea: A bigger voltage squeeze pushes more current through. But if you kink the hose (add resistance), less water flows out—and we need to squeeze harder to get the same flow.

The Mathematical Relationship

These three quantities are connected by Ohm's Law:

V = I × R

Voltage (V) = Current (I) × Resistance (R)

This means:

  • Higher voltage → more current flows (if R is constant)
  • Higher resistance → less current flows (if V is constant)
  • More current → higher voltage OR lower resistance

💡 The "Magic Triangle": Cover the value you want to find, and the other two tell you what to do.

[ V ]
─────
[ I ][ R ]

Deriving and Applying Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law comes from the definition of resistance as the ratio of voltage to current:

R = V / I ← Resistance is voltage divided by current

Rearranging:
V = I × R ← Voltage equals current times resistance
I = V / R ← Current equals voltage divided by resistance

Worked Example: Circuit Design

You want an LED that draws safely at 15 mA (0.015 A) from a 9V battery. What resistor do you need?

Given: V = 9V, I = 0.015 A
Find: R = ?

Solution: R = V / I = 9V / 0.015A = 600 Ω

→ Use a 470Ω resistor (nearest standard value; current will be ~19 mA, which is safe)

Why is this important? Every component has safe limits. LEDs typically max out at 20 mA. Resistors protect by limiting current. This is electrical engineering.

🔄 Direct Current vs. Alternating Current

DC (Direct Current)

  • Flows in ONE direction only
  • Source: batteries, solar cells
  • ✅ What we use in ALL project work

AC (Alternating Current)

  • Reverses direction rapidly
  • Source: mains supply (wall socket)
  • ⚠️ DANGEROUS — we do NOT use mains

🌱 For Our Smart Garden: Everything runs on 9V DC from a battery—as safe as a TV remote control battery. We NEVER touch mains voltage in this lab.

🛠️ The Build: Your Power Rail

Today, you are building the foundation of your Smart Garden: a stable 9V power supply with a visual indicator (LED). This Power Rail will stay on your board for the entire 9-week project. Do it right.

📋 Materials Checklist

You will need:

1× Prototype board (breadboard)
1× 9V battery + clip
1× SPST switch
1× Red LED
1× 470Ω resistor
2× Short jumper wires (red & black)

Step-by-Step Instructions

Step 1: Orient Your Board

Place the prototype board horizontally. Identify the two long horizontal rails along the top and bottom edges. These will be your power buses.

Step 2: Connect the Power Rail

Connect the red wire from your battery's positive (+) terminal to the top rail (label: +9V in pencil)

Connect the black wire from the negative (−) terminal to the bottom rail (label: 0V / GND)

Step 3: Insert the Switch

Place your SPST switch so it bridges across the gap in the middle of the board.

Connect one side to the +9V rail using a short jumper wire.

Step 4: Build the LED Indicator

Insert the 470Ω resistor from the switch output into the working area.

Insert the LED with its long leg (anode = +) into the resistor's output row.

Insert the short leg (cathode = −) into the 0V rail.

⚠️ Safety Checkpoint: BEFORE connecting the battery, ask your partner: "Is the LED in the right way? Long leg toward +, short leg toward 0V?"

Step 5: Connect and Test

Attach the 9V battery clip. Flip the switch ON. The red LED should light up.

If it doesn't light: Check LED polarity. Check all connections are seated firmly.

Step 6: Verify with Ohm's Law

Using a multimeter set to DC Voltage:

  • Measure across the battery: should read ~9V ✅
  • Measure across the LED: should read ~2V ✅
  • Measure across the resistor: should read ~7V ✅

Why? 9V − 2V (LED) = 7V across the resistor. Using Ohm's Law: I = 7V ÷ 470Ω ≈ 15 mA. That's exactly what we expect. You just proved Ohm's Law with your own hands!

Past-Paper Style Question (IGCSE 4.4.1)

Question

A component in a circuit allows a current of 0.25 A to flow through it when it is connected to a 12 V supply.

(a) Using Ohm's Law, calculate the resistance of the component. Show your working. (2 marks)

(b) State the unit of resistance. (1 mark)

(c) Calculate the electrical power dissipated by the component. (Hint: P = V × I) (2 marks)

Total: 5 marks

🔑 Answer Key (For Self-Checking)

Click to reveal answer key

(a) R = V ÷ I = 12V ÷ 0.25A = 48Ω (2 marks)

(b) Ohm (Ω) (1 mark)

(c) P = V × I = 12V × 0.25A = 3W (2 marks)
