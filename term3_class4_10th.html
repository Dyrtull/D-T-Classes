🔄 What is a Transducer?

A transducer is an electronic device that converts energy from one form to another.

Transducer Senses Converts To
LDR Light level Changing resistance
Thermistor (NTC) Temperature Changing resistance

💬 They don't generate electricity. They don't move. They simply change their resistance—and that resistance change is what we measure using the potential divider we already know.

👁️ The LDR: The Garden's Eye

An LDR's resistance decreases as light increases—and vice versa. It's like a shy person: in a bright crowd, they open up. In the dark, they close off.

LDR Behaviour Table

Light Condition LDR Resistance
Bright sunlight ~120Ω (very low)
Dim indoor light ~10kΩ (medium)
Completely covered (dark) Up to 10MΩ (very high)

💬 The Key Pattern: More light → Lower resistance. Less light → Higher resistance.

LDR Symbol: A resistor rectangle with two inward arrows (representing incoming light)

Why the LDR Changes Resistance

The LDR contains a semiconductor material (usually cadmium sulphide) whose electrical properties change when exposed to light. Photons of light knock electrons loose, increasing conductivity and DECREASING resistance.

This means:

  • More photons hitting the material = more free electrons = lower resistance
  • Fewer photons (darkness) = fewer free electrons = higher resistance
  • The change happens instantly (in milliseconds)
  • The LDR can sense light/dark cycles throughout the day

Typical range: 50Ω in bright sunlight to 10MΩ (that's 10,000,000Ω!) in complete darkness. A 200,000× change!

🌡️ The Thermistor: The Garden's Skin

A thermistor is a resistor whose value changes with temperature. The NTC type (Negative Temperature Coefficient) is what we use: As temperature increases, resistance decreases.

Thermistor Behaviour Table

Temperature Condition NTC Thermistor Resistance
Cold (~10°C) High resistance
Room temp (~25°C) Medium resistance
Hot (~40°C) Low resistance

💬 The Key Pattern: More heat → Lower resistance. Less heat → Higher resistance. (Same as LDR with light!)

Medellín Context: Temperatures in Medellín range from ~12°C at night to ~28°C during the day. Your thermistor will respond to these variations to control watering and cooling.

Why NTC Thermistors Work

NTC thermistors are made from semiconductor materials (usually metal oxides) whose conductivity increases dramatically with temperature. Heat gives electrons more energy to move freely.

The resistance-temperature relationship:

  • Hotter temperature = more thermal energy = more free electrons = lower resistance
  • Cooler temperature = less thermal energy = fewer free electrons = higher resistance
  • The relationship is nonlinear (not a straight line)
  • Response time: ~5-10 seconds for significant change

NTC Symbol: A resistor rectangle with a diagonal arrow and "-t°" label (the minus sign indicates Negative Temperature Coefficient)

⚡ The Live Sensor Formula

When we place an LDR as R₁ and a fixed resistor as R₂ in a potential divider, Vout changes automatically as light changes—no human adjustment needed.

The formula is EXACTLY the same as Week 3:

Vout = (R₂ / (RLDR + R₂)) × Vin

Three Real-World Scenarios (R₂ = 10kΩ, Vin = 9V)

🌞 Scenario 1: Bright Sunlight

LDR = 120Ω (very low in bright light)

Vout = (10,000 / (120 + 10,000)) × 9

= (10,000 / 10,120) × 9

= 0.988 × 9

≈ 8.89V
⛅ Scenario 2: Cloudy Afternoon

LDR = 5kΩ (medium in cloudy light)

Vout = (10,000 / (5,000 + 10,000)) × 9

= (10,000 / 15,000) × 9

= 0.667 × 9

= 6.0V
🌙 Scenario 3: After Dark

LDR = 50kΩ (very high in darkness)

Vout = (10,000 / (50,000 + 10,000)) × 9

= (10,000 / 60,000) × 9

= 0.167 × 9

= 1.5V

⭐ THE CRITICAL INSIGHT ⭐

As darkness falls, Vout falls from 8.89V all the way down to 1.5V. The transistor (in Week 7) only switches ON when Vout reaches 0.6V. So there's a specific darkness level where the system triggers. That threshold is your garden's "bedtime alarm."

A student builds a light-sensing circuit using an LDR and a fixed resistor of 10kΩ in series across a 9V supply. Output voltage (Vout) is taken from the junction between them.

(a) What is meant by the term "transducer"? (1 mark)

(b) Describe what happens to the LDR resistance as light increases. (1 mark)

(c) In bright sunlight, the LDR has 500Ω. Calculate Vout. Show working. (3 marks)

(d) The student covers the LDR completely. Describe and explain what happens to Vout. (2 marks)

(e) A variable resistor is added in series with the 10kΩ resistor. Explain why this improves the circuit. (2 marks)

Total: 9 marks

🔑 Answer Key

(a) An electronic device that converts energy from one form to another (1 mark)

(b) The LDR resistance decreases as light level increases (1 mark)

(c) Vout = (10,000 / (500 + 10,000)) × 9 = (10,000 / 10,500) × 9 ≈ 8.57V (3 marks)

(d) Vout decreases significantly; covering LDR greatly increases resistance, so R₂ takes smaller proportion, driving Vout down (2 marks)

(e) Variable resistor allows sensitivity adjustment—the exact light level at which circuit triggers can be fine-tuned (2 marks)
