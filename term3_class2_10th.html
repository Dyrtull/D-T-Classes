⚙️ What is a Resistor?

A fixed resistor is a component designed to limit the flow of electrical current in a circuit. Think of it like the nozzle on a garden hose: narrow nozzle (high resistance) restricts flow; wide nozzle (low resistance) lets more through.

Type What It Does Used in Our Garden?
Fixed Resistor Sets a precise, unchanging resistance ✅ YES — protects LED, transistor base
Variable Resistor (Potentiometer) Resistance changes when you turn a knob ✅ YES — sensitivity adjustment in sensor
Pre-set Resistor Set once with a screwdriver, then left alone ✅ YES — fine-tuning sensor threshold

💬 Key Idea: We choose the right "nozzle" (resistor value) depending on which component we're protecting. Different components need different resistances.

🎨 The Colour Code: The Secret Language of Resistors

Fixed resistors are identified by coloured bands printed on their body. These bands tell you the resistance value and tolerance (allowed error).

Reading the Four Bands

A 4-band resistor has:

  • Band 1: First digit of the value
  • Band 2: Second digit of the value
  • Band 3: Multiplier (how many zeros to add)
  • Band 4: Tolerance (allowed error range)
Colour Band 1 & 2 (Digit) Band 3 (Multiplier) Band 4 (Tolerance)
● Black 0 ×1 —
● Brown 1 ×10 ±1%
● Red 2 ×100 ±2%
● Orange 3 ×1,000 —
● Yellow 4 ×10,000 —
● Green 5 ×100,000 —
● Blue 6 ×1,000,000 —
● Violet 7 — —
● Grey 8 — —
● White 9 — —
● Gold — — ±5%
● Silver — — ±10%

(Black, Brown, Red, Orange, Yellow, Green, Blue, Violet, Grey, White)

Step-by-Step: Decoding a Resistor

Example: A resistor has bands: Yellow — Violet — Red — Gold

Step Band Colour Value
1st digit Band 1 Yellow 4
2nd digit Band 2 Violet 7
Multiplier Band 3 Red ×100
Tolerance Band 4 Gold ±5%

Calculation:

47 × 100 = 4,700Ω = 4.7kΩ ±5%

This means the actual resistance is somewhere between 4,465Ω and 4,935Ω (±5% of 4,700).

Why tolerance? Real-world resistors are never perfectly exact. Manufacturers can't guarantee an exact value, so they give you a range. A ±5% tolerance is very good for most circuits.

Advanced: Tolerance Calculations & Precision

When a resistor has tolerance ±5%, it means the actual resistance can vary by 5% above or below the nominal value.

Calculating Tolerance Range

Tolerance % = (tolerance value / 100) × nominal resistance

Example: 4.7kΩ ±5%
5% of 4,700 = 0.05 × 4,700 = 235Ω

Minimum = 4,700 − 235 = 4,465Ω
Maximum = 4,700 + 235 = 4,935Ω

Why This Matters in Design

When you measure a resistor's actual value with a multimeter, it should fall within this calculated range. If it doesn't, either:

  • The resistor is damaged or fake
  • Your multimeter is miscalibrated
  • You're measuring in a live circuit (never do this!)

✅ Best Practice: Always verify your colour code reading with a multimeter measurement. Theory meets practice here.

📊 The E12 Series: Standard Resistor Values

You can't buy a 4,823Ω resistor. Resistors only come in standard values called the E12 series:

10, 12, 15, 18, 22, 27, 33, 39, 47, 56, 68, 82

These repeat at every power of 10: ×1Ω, ×10Ω, ×100Ω, ×1,000Ω, ×10,000Ω...

💬 Design Rule: If your calculation says you need 725Ω, use the nearest E12 value: 680Ω. In practice, this tiny difference almost never matters.

Quick Practice

You Calculate You Need... Nearest E12 Value
95Ω 100Ω
430Ω 390Ω or 470Ω (use 390Ω)
2,200Ω 2,200Ω = 2.2kΩ ✅ (exact match!)
9,500Ω 10,000Ω = 10kΩ

📟 The Multimeter: Your Best Friend

💬 A multimeter is the stethoscope of an electronics engineer. A doctor puts a stethoscope on your chest to listen to your heart. We put a multimeter on a circuit to "listen" to what the electricity is doing.

Three Main Measurements

Measurement How to Connect Rule Symbol on Dial
Voltage (V) In PARALLEL with the component Voltmeter goes ACROSS V with ~ or —
Current (A) In SERIES — break the circuit Ammeter goes THROUGH A
Resistance (Ω) Component removed from circuit Measure on its own Ω (Omega)

⚠️ Critical Safety Rule

NEVER measure resistance on a live (powered) circuit. Always switch the power OFF first.

An incorrect resistance measurement on a powered circuit can permanently destroy your multimeter.

Multimeter Care Checklist

  • Handle with care — delicate and expensive
  • Never let it fall off the bench
  • Check the battery before use
  • Make sure you select the correct measurement range
  • Never trail test leads over bench edges
